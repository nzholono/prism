# When to Hire a Lawyer vs. Go It Alone

Most Illinois legal situations don't require a private attorney. Some do —
and getting it wrong is expensive in both directions (paying $300/hr for
something you could do free, or trying to handle a complex case yourself
and losing it).

## A rule of thumb

| Situation | Default |
| --- | --- |
| Small claims (under $10K) | **Pro se** (yourself) — fee-shifting and simplified procedure |
| Routine landlord-tenant | **Pro se** with free legal aid help |
| Employment discrimination | **IDHR first** (free), lawyer if it goes to court |
| Traffic ticket | **Pro se** unless points threaten license |
| Misdemeanor criminal | **Public defender** if can't afford, lawyer if you can |
| Felony criminal | **Always a lawyer.** Public defender if necessary |
| DUI | **Always a lawyer.** Stakes too high |
| Immigration | **Always a specialist.** Wrong move can be deportation |
| Estate planning | **Lawyer** unless very simple |
| Divorce with kids or significant assets | **Lawyer** |
| Personal injury serious enough to lose work | **Lawyer** (contingency — no upfront cost) |
| Anything you've been **sued** for above small claims | **Lawyer** |

## Why you can often go pro se

**Small-claims court is designed for pro se litigants.** No jury, no formal
rules of evidence, judge actively helps both sides explain themselves.
Filing fees are under $200. The Cook County clerk publishes step-by-step
guides.

**Administrative proceedings** (Illinois Department of Labor, Illinois
Department of Human Rights, Department of Administrative Hearings for
Chicago tickets) are also pro-se friendly. The agency investigates; you
provide evidence.

**Fee-shifting statutes** make this even better in some areas. The Illinois
Wage Payment Act, FDCPA, BIPA, Consumer Fraud Act, and Security Deposit
Return Act all let a prevailing **plaintiff** recover attorney's fees from
the defendant — which means private lawyers will take strong cases on
**contingency** (no payment unless you win).

## Why you should hire a lawyer

**Criminal cases.** Even a misdemeanor conviction can prevent employment,
licensing, and immigration relief for years. Public defenders are usually
overworked but skilled — request one immediately if you can't afford
private counsel.

**Felonies and DUI.** Consequences are severe and procedurally complex.
Always retain (or get appointed) counsel.

**Immigration.** Wrong moves can lead to deportation. Even seemingly
helpful things (like applying for a benefit you're not actually eligible
for) can trigger removal proceedings. Get a specialist.

**Civil cases where you're the defendant.** Plaintiffs have lawyers and a
case worked out. Defending pro se against a represented party is uphill.

**Complex business or estate matters.** The downside of mistakes
compounds for years.

**When the other side has a lawyer and you can afford one.** Lawyers
talk to lawyers more easily; cases settle faster when both sides are
represented.

## How to find a lawyer in Illinois (free or low-cost)

**Free legal aid (income-based, but generous thresholds):**

- **CARPLS** (Chicago Bar Foundation legal hotline): (312) 738-9200.
  First call for almost any Illinois legal question.
- **Illinois Legal Aid Online**: illinoislegalaid.org — interactive
  self-help tools, lawyer-locator, free template documents.
- **Chicago Volunteer Legal Services**: cvls.org — match with a
  volunteer attorney.
- **LAF (Legal Assistance Foundation of Metropolitan Chicago)**:
  lafchicago.org.

**Specialty free clinics:**

- **Cabrini Green Legal Aid** — criminal records, expungement, family.
- **Lawyers' Committee for Better Housing** — tenant cases.
- **National Immigrant Justice Center** — immigration.
- **Equip for Equality** — disability rights.
- **Equality Illinois** + **Lambda Legal** — LGBTQ+.
- **Transformative Justice Law Project** — trans Illinoisans.

**Lawyer referral services (low-cost first consultation, usually $25–$50
for 30 minutes):**

- **Illinois State Bar Association**: (800) 922-8757
- **Chicago Bar Association**: (312) 554-2001

**Contingency-fee lawyers** (no payment unless you win):

- Most personal injury cases.
- Many employment discrimination cases.
- Wage-theft cases.
- BIPA cases.
- Consumer fraud cases involving fee-shifting statutes.
- Personal injury.

## How to interview a lawyer

Before you sign an engagement, ask:

1. **How many cases like mine have you handled?**
2. **What outcomes did those cases reach?**
3. **What do you think the strengths and weaknesses of my case are?**
4. **What's your fee structure?** (Hourly? Flat? Contingency? Retainer?)
5. **What additional costs should I expect** (filing fees, expert
   witnesses, depositions)?
6. **Who in your office will be doing the actual work?**
7. **What's a realistic timeline?**
8. **What should I do — and not do — between now and our next meeting?**

## Red flags

- Demanding a large retainer for a simple case.
- Guaranteeing a specific outcome ("I'll get you $50,000").
- Not putting fee terms in writing.
- Hard sell — "Sign today or I can't help you."
- Won't return calls during the consultation.
- Specializes in everything (real estate, divorce, criminal, immigration,
  tax). Pick a specialist for non-trivial matters.

## When in doubt

**Call CARPLS first**: (312) 738-9200. They will tell you whether
you need a lawyer, refer you to a free clinic if you qualify, or
recommend a paid lawyer's referral service.
