# How Illinois Law Is Organized

A short, plain-English tour of the legal sources Prism cites — so when you
see "765 ILCS 710/1" or "Chi. Mun. Code 5-12-080" you know exactly what
you're looking at.

## Three layers, top to bottom

American law in Illinois operates in three layers:

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  FEDERAL                                   │
│  Constitution + U.S. Code (USC)            │
│  Federal regulations (CFR)                 │
│  Federal court decisions                   │
├────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  ILLINOIS                                  │
│  Illinois Constitution                     │
│  Illinois Compiled Statutes (ILCS)         │
│  Illinois Administrative Code              │
│  Illinois Supreme + Appellate decisions    │
├────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  LOCAL (Chicago, Cook County, etc.)        │
│  Municipal codes & county ordinances       │
└────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

When sources conflict, **higher beats lower** — federal beats state, state
beats local — *unless* the lower source gives you more protection. A common
pattern: Chicago's tenant protections (local) are stronger than Illinois's
(state), and so the Chicago RLTO controls in Chicago.

## Federal sources

### U.S. Code (USC)

The codified federal statutes. Citation format:

> **15 U.S.C. § 1692**

Read this as "Title 15 of the U.S. Code, section 1692". The Fair Debt
Collection Practices Act lives here.

### Code of Federal Regulations (CFR)

Regulations issued by federal agencies. Citation format:

> **8 CFR § 214.2(f)**

This means "Title 8 of the Code of Federal Regulations, section 214.2(f)".
F-1 student visa rules live here.

### Federal courts

Three levels: **District Courts** (trial), **Circuit Courts of Appeal** (Illinois is in the **7th Circuit**), and the **Supreme Court of the United States**. Their decisions interpret federal statutes and the Constitution.

## Illinois state sources

### Illinois Compiled Statutes (ILCS)

The codified state statutes. Citation format:

> **765 ILCS 710/1**

Read this as: **Chapter 765**, Act **710**, Section **1**. Chapter 765 is
the property-and-conveyances chapter; Act 710 is the Security Deposit Return
Act; Section 1 is its first section.

The full list of chapters at ilga.gov/legislation/ilcs/ilcs.asp:

- **Chapter 5**: Government
- **Chapter 20**: Executive Branch
- **Chapter 105**: Schools
- **Chapter 210**: Health Facilities
- **Chapter 410**: Public Health
- **Chapter 625**: Vehicles
- **Chapter 720**: Criminal Offenses
- **Chapter 725**: Criminal Procedure
- **Chapter 735**: Civil Procedure
- **Chapter 740**: Civil Liabilities
- **Chapter 750**: Families
- **Chapter 765**: Property
- **Chapter 775**: Human Rights
- **Chapter 815**: Business Transactions
- **Chapter 820**: Employment

(...and many more)

### Illinois courts

Three levels: **Circuit Courts** (trial — one per county, plus Cook County's
specialized divisions), **Appellate Court** (5 districts), and the
**Illinois Supreme Court**. Their decisions interpret Illinois statutes
and the Illinois Constitution.

A landmark Illinois case cited in Prism: *Jack Spring, Inc. v. Little*, 50
Ill.2d 351 (1972) — established the implied warranty of habitability.

Format: **<Plaintiff> v. <Defendant>, <volume> <reporter> <page> (<year>)**.
"50 Ill.2d 351" means volume 50 of *Illinois Reports, Second Series*,
page 351.

## Local sources

### Chicago Municipal Code

Citation format:

> **Chi. Mun. Code 5-12-080**

Read this as Chicago Municipal Code, chapter 5, section 12-080. Chapter 5
covers housing; 5-12 is the Chicago Residential Landlord and Tenant
Ordinance (RLTO); 5-12-080 is the section on security deposits.

The full Chicago code is at codelibrary.amlegal.com/codes/chicago.

### Cook County and other counties

Cook County has its own ordinances (separate from Chicago, which is inside
Cook County but a separate jurisdiction). DuPage, Lake, and other counties
have their own codes too. Less central to most everyday questions, but
sometimes relevant (e.g., suburban Cook County tenant protections fall
under Cook County's residential tenant ordinance).

## How to actually find a statute

1. **Start with Illinois Legal Aid Online** (illinoislegalaid.org). They
   write plain-English summaries that link to the statute.
2. **For the official statute text**, go to ilga.gov/legislation/ilcs.
3. **For Chicago ordinances**, codelibrary.amlegal.com/codes/chicago.
4. **For federal**, govinfo.gov or the unofficial-but-friendlier law.cornell.edu.
5. **For case law**, law.justia.com or scholar.google.com (set "Case law" filter).

## How to read a statute

A useful pattern:

1. **Read the title and the first section.** They usually define what the
   statute covers.
2. **Find the definitions section.** Legal statutes use technical
   definitions — "employee", "household member", "consumer" don't always
   mean what you think.
3. **Find the operative section** — the rules of behavior.
4. **Find the penalty / remedy section.** What happens if someone violates
   it? What can you recover?
5. **Find the procedure section.** How do you actually use this — file
   where, by when?

## Why citations matter

You don't need to memorize statute numbers — but when you're talking to a
landlord, employer, or government office, **citing the specific statute by
number signals you've done your homework**. Compare:

- "I think you have to return my deposit within a month or so?"
- "Per 765 ILCS 710/1, you have 45 days to return the deposit and 30 days
  for an itemized statement of any deductions."

The second response gets responses; the first one gets ignored.
